Yes. A strategy that **always chooses the action with the highest immediate reward** (a *greedy* strategy) can perform worse than strategies that sometimes accept a smaller immediate reward to achieve larger future rewards.

The key reason is that in reinforcement learning, the objective is to **maximize the cumulative (expected) reward over time**, not just the reward received on the current step.

### Example

Suppose an agent starts in state **S** and has two actions:

* **Action A**

  * Immediate reward: **+10**
  * Transitions to a terminal state (episode ends).

* **Action B**

  * Immediate reward: **+2**
  * Leads to another state where the agent can receive **+20** on the next step.

The total rewards are:

* Greedy strategy:

  * Choose **A** → Total reward = **10**

* Far-sighted strategy:

  * Choose **B** → Total reward = **2 + 20 = 22**

Although Action A gives the highest reward in the current state, it leads to a lower overall return.

### Stochastic rewards

The same idea applies when rewards are stochastic. For example:

* Action A:

  * Always gives reward **8**.

* Action B:

  * Gives reward **0** immediately, but leads to a state where the expected future reward is **15**.

The expected return of Action B is still higher, even though its immediate reward is lower.

### Why reinforcement learning doesn't simply maximize immediate reward

Reinforcement learning algorithms evaluate actions using their **expected return**, typically defined as

<Math display latex="G_t = R_{t+1} + \gamma R_{t+2} + \gamma^2 R_{t+3} + \cdots," />

where:

* <Math latex="R_{t+i}" /> is the reward received <Math latex="i" /> steps in the future.
* <Math latex="\gamma" /> (the discount factor) determines how much future rewards are valued.

The optimal policy chooses actions that maximize this expected cumulative return, not the immediate reward.
